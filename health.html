The AQI Scale Explained

The US EPA Air Quality Index (AQI) runs from 0 to 500. Higher values indicate greater levels of air pollution and health concern.

Good
AQI 0 – 50

Air quality is satisfactory, and air pollution poses little or no risk to the general population.

Enjoy outdoor activities freely
Open windows for ventilation
No precautions needed
Moderate
AQI 51 – 100

Acceptable air quality. A small number of people who are unusually sensitive to air pollution may experience minor discomfort.

Outdoor activities are generally fine
Sensitive individuals should limit prolonged outdoor exertion
Keep windows open unless symptomatic
Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups
AQI 101 – 150

Sensitive groups (children, elderly, people with asthma or heart conditions) may experience health effects. The general public is less likely to be affected.

Sensitive groups should reduce outdoor exertion
Keep inhalers or medication accessible
General public: normal activity is OK
Unhealthy
AQI 151 – 200

Everyone may begin to experience adverse health effects. Sensitive groups will be more severely affected.

Avoid prolonged outdoor activity
Wear an N95/KN95 mask outdoors
Use air purifiers indoors
Sensitive groups should stay indoors
Very Unhealthy
AQI 201 – 300

Health alert — the risk of health effects is increased for everyone. Serious effects expected in sensitive groups.

Stay indoors as much as possible
N95 mask is essential if outdoors
Run air purifiers and keep windows closed
Seek medical care if experiencing symptoms
Hazardous
AQI 301 – 500

Health warning of emergency conditions. The entire population is more likely to be affected by serious health effects.

Do NOT go outdoors
Seal gaps in doors and windows
Emergency services may be needed
Follow official emergency instructions

Who Is Most at Risk?

Certain groups are more vulnerable to the effects of air pollution and should take additional precautions even at moderate AQI levels.

Children

Lungs are still developing. Higher breathing rates mean more pollutants inhaled per body weight. Avoid outdoor sports on high-AQI days.

Elderly Adults

Reduced immune response and lung capacity make older adults more susceptible. Cardiovascular and respiratory risks increase significantly.

Heart Conditions

Fine particles (PM2.5) can enter the bloodstream, triggering inflammation and increasing the risk of heart attacks and strokes.

Asthma & COPD

Pollutants like ozone and PM2.5 can trigger asthma attacks and worsen COPD. Always carry a rescue inhaler on high-pollution days.

Outdoor Athletes

Exercise increases breathing rate 10–20x, dramatically increasing pollutant intake. Check AQI before outdoor workouts.

Pregnant Women

Air pollution exposure during pregnancy is linked to preterm birth and low birth weight. Extra caution is advised during polluted days.

Key Pollutants Explained

Breathe tracks six major air pollutants. Here's what each one is and why it matters for your health.

PM2.5

Fine Particulate Matter (PM2.5)

Tiny particles less than 2.5 µm in diameter. They penetrate deep into lung tissue and enter the bloodstream. The most dangerous pollutant for long-term health. WHO safe level: 5 µg/m³ (annual).

PM10

Coarse Particulate Matter (PM10)

Particles between 2.5 and 10 µm. Includes dust, pollen, and mold. Can cause irritation of the nose, throat, and airways. WHO safe level: 15 µg/m³ (annual).

NO₂

Nitrogen Dioxide (NO₂)

Mainly from vehicle emissions and power plants. Irritates the respiratory system and contributes to the formation of ozone and PM2.5. WHO safe level: 10 µg/m³ (annual).

O₃

Ozone (O₃)

Ground-level ozone is formed when sunlight reacts with NOx and VOCs. Causes chest pain, coughing, and worsens asthma. Peaks on hot, sunny days. WHO safe level: 60 µg/m³ (8-hour).

SO₂

Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂)

Released by burning coal and oil. Causes respiratory irritation and contributes to acid rain. People with asthma are particularly sensitive. WHO safe level: 40 µg/m³ (24-hour).

CO

Carbon Monoxide (CO)

Produced by incomplete combustion of fuels. Reduces the blood's ability to carry oxygen. At high concentrations can be fatal. Common near heavy traffic. WHO safe level: 10 mg/m³ (8-hour).

General Protection Strategies

Simple everyday actions that significantly reduce your exposure to air pollution.

Wear an N95/KN95 Mask

Properly fitted N95 or KN95 masks filter out 95%+ of airborne particles. Essential when AQI exceeds 150.

Use an Air Purifier

HEPA air purifiers significantly reduce indoor PM2.5 levels. Keep windows closed and the purifier running during high-pollution days.

Time Your Outdoor Activities

Pollution is often lowest in the early morning. Avoid peak traffic hours (7–9 AM, 5–7 PM) for outdoor exercise.

Add Indoor Plants

Plants like Peace Lily, Spider Plant, and Boston Fern can help filter some indoor air pollutants and improve humidity.

Reduce Driving

Opt for public transit, cycling, or walking. Reducing vehicle use is one of the highest-impact actions for cleaner air at a community level.

Stay Hydrated

Drinking adequate water helps your respiratory system flush out particulate matter and keeps mucous membranes functioning well.

AQI is a standardized, dimensionless index (0–500) calculated from raw pollutant concentrations (µg/m³ or ppm). It translates complex measurements into a single easy-to-understand number.
